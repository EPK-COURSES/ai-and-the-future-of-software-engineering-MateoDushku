## What surprised me most?

What surprised me most during this research was how quickly the definition of "being a good developer" is shifting. I expected AI tools to be useful helpers, but I did not expect that the highest-valued skills are already moving away from syntax and toward things like critical thinking, system design, and communication. The Stack Overflow 2025 survey finding — that 46% of developers distrust AI output compared to only 33% who trust it — was striking. Even as AI usage is growing rapidly, experienced engineers are becoming *more* skeptical, not less. That tells me that technical judgment is becoming more important, not obsolete.

---

## What concerns me about AI in software engineering?

My main concern is what happens to the learning process for new developers. The Thoughtworks article raised something I think is genuinely serious: when AI tools abstract away the difficulty of coding, they may also abstract away the understanding that comes from struggling through problems. The concern that junior developers are "missing the foundational knowledge" because they can get a working solution without understanding it feels very real to me as a student. If I use AI to complete every exercise without understanding the output, I might graduate with a portfolio of code I cannot explain or maintain.

I am also concerned about over-reliance on AI in systems where errors have real consequences. AI hallucinations — where the model produces code that looks correct but contains subtle logical flaws — are a serious risk that is easy to underestimate when the output "looks right."
---

## What opportunities do I see?

The biggest opportunity is that AI tools can dramatically accelerate the parts of development that are repetitive or boilerplate, which means more time for the work that actually requires human judgment — architecture, problem framing, and creative problem solving. For developers who invest in understanding how to collaborate effectively with AI, the productivity gains are real. This research reinforced that the opportunity is not about replacing effort, but about redirecting it.

There is also an interesting opportunity in the field of AI literacy itself. As organizations struggle with the skills gap that Gartner identified, people who can bridge AI capabilities and software engineering practice — who understand both systems design and how to work with LLMs — will be highly valuable.

## Which skills do I think will remain most important?

Based on my research, I believe the following will remain central:

1. **Critical thinking and code review** — AI can write code but cannot reliably judge if it is correct or appropriate for a specific system. Human review is irreplaceable.
2. **System design** — Understanding how large systems are structured, how components interact, and how to build for reliability is difficult for AI to replicate at a professional level.
3. **Communication** — The ability to translate between business needs and technical implementation remains distinctly human.
4. **Strong programming fundamentals** — Prompt engineering and AI literacy build on top of real understanding. Engineers who skip the fundamentals will struggle to catch AI mistakes.

## How will I personally adapt?

This research changed how I think about my own studies. I will continue to use AI tools, but more carefully — I plan to treat AI-generated code as a first draft that I need to understand, not a final answer I can submit. When I use GitHub Copilot or ChatGPT to help with an assignment, I want to be able to explain every line.

I also plan to invest time in understanding the basics of how LLMs work, not just how to use them. And I want to focus on system design and architecture — not just writing functions, but understanding how software systems are structured at a higher level.

The most important takeaway for me personally is that the engineers who will remain valuable are those who combine strong fundamentals with the ability to work with AI critically and purposefully. That is the kind of engineer I want to be.
